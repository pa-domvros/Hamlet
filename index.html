Sadness vs. Clinical depression — interactive help

Show quick comparison

Sadness

  • Often linked to a specific event
  • Short-term; lifts as circumstances change
  • Doesn’t seriously affect daily functioning

Clinical depression

  • Medical disorder; with/without a clear trigger
  • Lasts ≥ 2 weeks
  • Significantly interferes with ability to work, play, or love

Model ideas:
  1. When Hamlet sits heavily and reaches toward the pill bottle (~150s), this mirrors the TED-Ed symptom loss of energy plus the reference to recurrent thoughts of suicide.
  2. The penknife (“bare bodkin”) moment underlines restlessness/slowness shifting to intent, connecting to how depression can affect the ability to work, play, or love.
  3. His downcast gaze and tightened jaw during “To die, to sleep” align with low mood and poor concentration described in the TED-Ed clip.

Lesson Plan — Hamlet & Depression

Lesson Plan — “Hamlet & Depression: Understanding Choices”

Level: B2 English (Greek students) Class Size: 25 students (5 groups of 5) Framework: Luke & Freebody’s Four Resources Model Total Time: 40 minutes Final Digital Tools Used: Integrated HTML lesson guide with embedded videos, interactive non-verbal analysis, drag-and-drop symptoms, dropdown selectors, model answer unlock, print/save as PDF, language bank with copy buttons, and feedback form.

Lesson Flow

1) First Impressions — Hook (5 min)

Resource Role: Meaning Maker

  1. Display two still screenshots from the soliloquy (neutral face and heightened emotion).
  2. Students discuss in pairs: “What emotions can you see?”
  3. Collect quick ideas on board: sadness, fear, hesitation, hopelessness.
  4. Teacher explains: “We’re going to see how acting choices and modern mental health ideas connect in Hamlet.”

2) While You Watch — Non-Verbal Noticing Guide (8 min)

Resource Role: Code Breaker + Meaning Maker

  1. Play full Ben Whishaw “To be or not to be” performance (video embedded at top of HTML).
  2. Students work in interactive group form:
  3. Dropdown group selection and calendar date picker auto-record inputs.
  4. Use provided input boxes to note:
  5. Facial expression changes (moment + description).
  6. Posture & gesture meaning.
  7. Prop symbolism (water bottle + pills, penknife “bodkin” reference).
  8. They can pause video and type notes directly in the guide.

 

3) Modern Context — TED-Ed “What is Depression?” (6 min)

Resource Role: Code Breaker

  1. Play first 1:28 of Helen M. Farrell’s TED-Ed video inside matching styled HTML container with synced subtitles.
  2. Interactive help popup explains Sadness vs Clinical Depression.
  3. Students complete a drag-and-drop activity from a symptom bank:
  4. Immediate feedback (green = correct, red = incorrect).
  5. Class discussion: “Which symptoms might match Hamlet’s behaviour?”

4) Screenshot Activity — Classify & Explain (4 min)

Resource Role: Text User

  1. Students examine three provided screenshots from the performance.
  2. In pairs, classify each according to symptom category (e.g., loss of energy, hopelessness, indecision).
  3. Immediate feedback after classification.
  4. Students type short explanations — option to save as PDF.

5) Connect the Texts — Three Clear Links (8 min)

Resource Role: Text User + Text Analyst

  1. Interactive table: For each of three links:
  2. Select a moment from Hamlet’s performance.
  3. Choose a related symptom from dropdown (TED-Ed bank).
  4. Add explanation (with expandable sentence starter hints).
  5. Live completeness check: cells turn green when both parts are entered.
  6. Model Answer unlock for teacher (double-click, password = teacher).

6) Language Bank — Functional Phrases (3 min)

Resource Role: Text User

  1. Students see a curated bank of performance analysis and debate phrases.
  2. Copy buttons for each phrase make it easy to paste into group review writing.

7) Mini-Review — Your Group Verdict (4 min)

Resource Role: Text Analyst

  1. Each group writes a short verdict on the director’s choices (modern props, mood, mental health connection).
  2. They type it into the HTML box.
  3. Print / Save as PDF button to submit or keep.

8) Feedback & Reflection (2 min)

Resource Role: Meaning Maker

  1. Students fill in integrated Tailwind-styled feedback form:
  2. Name, 5-star ratings for lesson aspects, and open comments.
  3. Submits via Formspree to teacher.

 

This Plan

  1. Keeps Shakespeare intact but shifts focus to performance, not language decoding.
  2. Modern mental health link creates real-world relevance.
  3. Multiple interactive modes: video annotation, drag-and-drop, dropdown selection, direct input, print/save.
  4. Five clear group work phases ensure all 25 students are active.
  5. Four Resources model ensures a balanced approach: decoding, meaning-making, text use, and critical analysis.
